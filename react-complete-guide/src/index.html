2:51: Wave’s company vision

Will: In your words, explain Wave’s vision.

Ideshini:

Well, I would say that there are three distinct parts to Wave’s vision. The first is that we want to set small business owners free to be the best version of themselves. And we want to be able to do that by automating away the mundane tasks that keep them from focusing on their profitable activity. That’s the very part.

The second thing that we want to be able to do is to help these business owners make better decisions, faster. And, we believe that we can do that for them by giving them insights into their business that help them understand exactly what’s going on at a very-grained level of detail, without them having to put literally hundreds of hours into analyzing the nitty-gritty details of how their business is working. By being able to do that, we’re empowering them with information, with knowledge that helps them beat the odds that their business will not survive, and we know that those odds are hard. We want to be able to give our small business owners those insights that help them beat those odds. But not just beat the odds and survive, but go on and be even better–thrive. Bevome valuable contributors to the economies and communities that they are part of, become increasingly valuable entities that employee people. So, those are the–that’s the greater value that we want to be able to unlock.

And the third piece that we think is incredibly important is being able to help small business owners get access to reliable market intel. They very rarely have that. And for them to be able to learn from best-of-breeds so that they can improve their own business practices is difficult. If a small business owner is approaching their accountant to get feedback on how their business is performing relative to competitors in their space, regardless of how talented their accountant is, chances are they’re going to give them a contrast to two or three of their other customers. We can provide those constrasts at the levels of tens of thousands of other small business owners practicing perhaps in the same field, in the same geography, with the same seasonal conditions–that’s the kind of reliable intel that we can give them to help them hone their business to be more successful.

5:28: How Wave Delivery supports Wave’s mission

Will: And how does Wave Delivery support this mission?

Ideshini:

Across–across Wave engineering, we’re setting ourselves up with both short, medium, and long-term capabilities that will enable this vision to come to life. On the short-term scale, we’re making sure that we’re able to respond quickly and with high quality to the immediate needs that are coming out of this market and that our customers need our help on, while simultaneously watching how those needs are changing and adapting to market conditions, so that we can respond fast when their needs change, and we have appropriate solutions for it. At the same time, we’re looking ahead and saying: How do we help our organization completely reimagine this space? How do we make the concepts of accounting, and payroll, and invoicing–how do we make these concepts disapper so that all the business owner has to do is engage with a highly intelligent piece of software that is capable of taking on most of those tasks for them, and they can focus on the things that require higher-order intelligence that the software can’t provide for them. Wave Engineering is making sure that all three of those are being fulfilled on a daily a basis.

So if they show up at a customer’s place of business, and they need relevant details about what job they’re going to be doing there, we can push it to them instead of having them have to pull it from external system. 19:29: How the interview process works

Will: What should a candidate coming to Wave expect from the interview process?

Debo:

Ah, so this is something that is near and dear to my heart, so–when I showed up at Wave four or five years ago–I can’t remember, four or five years ago, I had spent some time being a recruiter. Which, usually, is a pretty quick way to get somebody to stop talking to me if they’re an engineer. It was something that got me a lot of experience, going into over a hundred different startups learning how they worked, and helping many many engineers get through many interview processes and hopefully to a job that they loved. And that the thing that I sort of really connected with in that time was that that experience is really stressful on the candidate. And, I really didn’t like seeing that being a key component of so many interview processes where, the candidate may not know what stage they’re at, if they’re doing well or not, what to expect from the process. Maybe they come out of it and they don’t actually understand what they’re even going to be doing in their job and now they’re faced to sort of make a decision with what they’re going to do with the next big chunk of time in their life not really understanding the nuance or the details behind that decision.

So, coming from that background I had some key principles that we built into the process here from the beginning. And, you can kind of underscore it just by saying, let’s be good people to the people who come in here and give us their time and their effort to take a crack at potentially becoming a Waver.

So the kinds of ways that embeds itself in our recruiting process is that we’re really concerned about speed, so how quickly from the first time that we chat with you that you are able to get here and learn what it’s like to work here. The second is to deeply inform people who come through the process about what it’s like to work at Wave. So we do try to build our interview process so that you’re doing meaningful work in those sessions that sorta, kinda reflect what your day-to-day life would be here, so that when you’re going to make a decision one way or another, you’re not having to guess. You can be pretty clear about what it’s like to work with Wavers on the ground day-to-day. And then the last principle that we use throughout is making the process collaborative. And that’s not a lip-service word. What I mean there is a lot of times when any of us have gone through interview processes it feels a little bit adversarial. Maybe the people you were talking to before you show up at the interview are perfectly nice humans, they sound great, and then you sit down in the interview room and it suddenly feels like a competitive sport. That’s–some element of that is fine, but it’s not really what we’re going towards here. We really want each interview session to feel sort of like it’s a group effort or group decision and that’s not just for feels. Collaboration is actually a legitmately super important thing here at Wave. I’ve worked at a variety of software companies where that wasn’t necessarily true. I can see it here on the ground every day at Wave where problems from small to large are tackled by groups of smart people all the time. So we do want that to be part of the experience so that you again get an idea of what it’s like to work here at Wave on a day-to-day basis and that’s what it’s like. Those are some of the key principles in going through the recruiting process.

In terms of the structure, the way that looks depending on what technical role you’re applying for–there’s a common structure to it, but it’s going to vary depending on whether you’re interviewing for, for example–experience engineer, or platform engineer, or infrastructure engineer, or maybe staff engineer–some of the more senior roles. But the gist of it usually looks like some offsite exercise–not always writing a full code challenge or something like that, but maybe you do a code review that we can review later. And the reason why we do that is to get you in a position where you start to deeply understand a problem or a solution you might have to a problem so that you know it a little bit better than us. And that aids in that collaboration part when you get here. Because when you do get to the onsite, almost always you’ll be using part of that artifact you built offsite in the interview process. Either going over it with somebody here, or expanding on it, building on it, and that knowledge that you picked up doing that puts you in a bit of position of expertise so that it’s not always the interviewers know better than the interviewee. And that’s our design goal there.

At the onsite there’s usually two technical-oriented sessions. One that’s more systems-level oriented, higher-level thinking, and then one that’s more practice-oriented, maybe hands-on-keyboard. And then you’ll get a chance to talk about Wave’s values with usually a cross-section of Wavers, not necessarily people from engineering. And that’s about it. So usually within 24-48 hours of that onsite, we’ll have an answer for you. And that, that is our SLA–48 hours, so if we’re not making that, we have something to answer to. Yeah, I think that pretty much, underscores the whole process.
